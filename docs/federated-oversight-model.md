# HC:// Federated Oversight and Accountability Governance Model

This document defines a federated oversight and accountability governance model for HC:// within HC-TRUST-LAYER.

Scope boundaries:

- Documentation-only model.
- Advisory-only verification posture.
- No canonical schema changes.
- No validator or guard weakening.
- Human-supervised validation remains required.
- No autonomous machine-only authority claims.

## Purpose

Define how HC:// can evolve into a transparent, multi-layer, independently reviewable trust and verification ecosystem without relying on a single centralized authority.

## Governance concepts

### internal oversight

Internal oversight is the repository-governed review process operated by maintainers and assigned reviewers who preserve trust-kernel boundaries, provenance continuity, and documented review rules.

### external oversight

External oversight is independent review participation from parties outside the core maintainer set to reduce concentrated control risk and improve public accountability.

### federated review

Federated review is a multi-party review pattern where multiple independent organizations or review groups inspect the same verification-relevant evidence and compare conclusions.

### independent auditors

Independent auditors are recognized external reviewers who provide challengeable, traceable assessments that do not replace canonical record boundaries or human-supervised validation.

### validator accountability

Validator accountability means validator outputs must remain attributable, reviewable, and challengeable through validator trace continuity and auditable review actions.

### public auditability

Public auditability means verification-relevant review signals, policy revisions, and escalation outcomes should remain inspectable through documented audit trail references.

### multi-country and multi-organization review

Multi-country and multi-organization review distributes interpretive review across jurisdictional and organizational boundaries to reduce single-jurisdiction capture risk.

### distributed accountability

Distributed accountability means responsibility for consequential trust interpretation is shared across distinct reviewer roles and institutions rather than concentrated in one actor.

## Protected principles

- no single unchecked authority
- no hidden verification logic
- no unverifiable model-only authority
- traceable review actions
- transparent policy evolution
- review escalation paths
- public trust through inspectability

## Threat and risk models

### centralized capture

- Risk: one institution or maintainer cluster can steer outcomes without meaningful challenge.
- Impact: accountability erosion and hidden authority concentration.

### compromised maintainer

- Risk: privileged maintainer credentials or influence are abused to distort review outcomes.
- Impact: trust-kernel boundary misuse and audit trail manipulation pressure.

### fake external auditor

- Risk: a party claims independent auditor status without verifiable identity or review continuity.
- Impact: false legitimacy signals and review confusion.

### coordinated validator abuse

- Risk: multiple validators coordinate biased outputs to simulate consensus.
- Impact: challenge pathways are suppressed and uncertainty becomes obscured.

### political pressure

- Risk: state or institutional pressure attempts to force selective verification narratives.
- Impact: reduced neutrality and constrained reviewer independence.

### commercial influence

- Risk: sponsors or market actors push favorable interpretations without transparent disclosure.
- Impact: public trust degradation and opaque incentive distortion.

### hidden moderation

- Risk: material review actions or policy-routing changes occur without visible audit trace.
- Impact: unverifiable oversight claims and reduced inspectability.

### opaque AI decision-making

- Risk: AI-assisted outputs are treated as authoritative without validator trace and human-supervised validation.
- Impact: unverifiable authority inflation and accountability gaps.

## Defense approaches

- public audit logs for material review actions and escalation outcomes
- signed reviewer identity and role disclosure for consequential review events
- validator traceability requirements for AI-assisted and automated analysis outputs
- policy versioning with visible change rationale and review provenance
- federation cross-checks across independent review participants
- review conflict escalation paths with documented resolution handling
- independent oversight participation in high-impact or contested interpretations
- immutable-style audit continuity to preserve chronology and challengeability

## Future ecosystem examples

Potential participants in a federated oversight ecosystem include:

- academic auditors
- public-interest organizations
- independent technical reviewers
- AI providers with traceable validator disclosures
- regional validator groups
- civil oversight participants

These examples describe participation models, not automatic trust grants. Each participant remains subject to challenge, auditability, and human-supervised validation boundaries.

## Interpretation guidance

### HC:// should not require blind trust

HC:// should be interpretable through verifiable evidence, review trace continuity, and explicit uncertainty markers rather than authority-only assertions.

### systems must remain inspectable

Verification and oversight systems must expose enough process evidence for third parties to inspect methodology, boundaries, and escalation outcomes.

### trust signals must remain challengeable

Trust signals should always include challenge paths so reviewers and external participants can contest disputed interpretations.

### uncertainty must remain visible

Uncertainty, ambiguity, and incomplete continuity should remain explicit in state signals and reviewer-facing summaries.

### verification systems themselves require oversight

The verification layer, reviewer actions, and policy evolution processes require ongoing oversight to prevent hidden concentration of power.

## Implementation notes

- This model is documentation guidance and does not alter canonical record surfaces, schema contracts, validator logic, federation behavior, signing semantics, policy evaluator behavior, or workflow security controls.
- This model preserves advisory-only verification and human-supervised validation as mandatory boundaries for consequential interpretation.
