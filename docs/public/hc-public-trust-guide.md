# HC:// Public Trust and Verification Guide

This guide explains HC:// trust and verification in plain language for people who are not technical specialists.

Scope boundaries:

- Advisory-only verification is preserved.
- Human-supervised validation remains required.
- No canonical schema changes.
- No validator or guard weakening.
- No truth-guarantee or absolute-security claims.

## What HC:// is

HC:// is a verification and provenance framework in HC-TRUST-LAYER. It helps people inspect how evidence was checked, where it came from, and how review decisions were made.

HC:// is designed to support trust decisions through visibility and traceability, not hidden authority.

## What verification means

In HC://, verification means checking whether evidence appears consistent, traceable, and reviewable against defined boundaries.

Verification is about quality of evidence and process integrity. It is not the same as proving final certainty.

## What advisory verification means

Advisory verification means results are guidance for human review.

A result can help you decide what to inspect next, but it does not replace human-supervised validation for consequential decisions.

## What trust signals mean

Trust signals are visible indicators that help you interpret verification outcomes.

Examples include:

- whether provenance links are present
- whether audit trail continuity is available
- whether disputes are open
- whether reviewer decisions are documented

Signals help people compare confidence levels. They are not automatic proof.

## What disputes and challenges mean

Disputes and challenges are formal ways to question a verification outcome.

If people find conflicting evidence, missing continuity, or suspicious activity, they can challenge the result so reviewers can re-check the evidence.

Visible disputes protect accountability and reduce silent errors.

## Why uncertainty may remain visible

Sometimes evidence is incomplete, conflicting, or still under review. HC:// keeps that uncertainty visible instead of hiding it.

Visible uncertainty helps users avoid overconfidence and encourages careful decision-making.

## Why traceability matters

Traceability means you can follow how a result was produced, what evidence was used, and who reviewed it.

Without traceability, people may trust outcomes they cannot inspect.

With traceability, people can audit the path, identify weak points, and request corrections when needed.

## Why screenshots alone may not be trustworthy

A screenshot can be edited, taken out of context, or shown without underlying provenance.

HC:// emphasizes inspectable evidence links, continuity context, and audit trail visibility because images alone may not prove where information came from.

## Why public audit visibility matters

Public audit visibility lets communities inspect important trust decisions.

When audit signals are visible, hidden overrides are harder, accountability is stronger, and review quality improves over time.

## Simple concept explanations

### Canonical record

A canonical record is the official repository-defined record reference used for continuity and verification boundaries.

### Audit trail

An audit trail is the trace of key actions, evidence references, and review steps across time.

### Federation review

Federation review means multiple independent reviewers or validator groups can inspect and challenge verification outcomes.

### Reviewer accountability

Reviewer accountability means reviewer actions should be visible, attributable, and open to oversight.

### Continuity gap

A continuity gap means expected evidence links or sequence continuity are missing or broken.

### Evidence missing

Evidence missing means required supporting material is unavailable, incomplete, or cannot be verified within scope.

### Consensus not reached

Consensus not reached means reviewers do not yet agree on the interpretation of available evidence.

## Safety explanations

- HC:// reduces risk through inspectable process controls; it does not guarantee truth.
- Verification should remain inspectable so users can independently review trust signals.
- No single AI system or person should silently control trust outcomes.
- Users should remain cautious of manipulated content, especially when provenance is unclear.

## User guidance

Before trusting a result:

1. Verify before trusting.
2. Inspect dispute and challenge status.
3. Review traceability and continuity signals.
4. Understand where uncertainty remains visible.
5. Challenge suspicious verification results for re-review.

## Public philosophy

HC:// public trust philosophy in HC-TRUST-LAYER:

- trust through traceability
- visibility over hidden authority
- accountability before automation
- inspectable verification
