# HC:// Foundation Stable Candidate Definition

> **Documentation Status**
> - **status:** DRAFT
> - **scope:** Foundation Layer stable-candidate criteria for transition planning toward Operational Core development in HC-TRUST-LAYER.
> - **canonical relevance:** Advisory-only governance and architecture guidance; not a canonical record surface.
> - **runtime relevance:** Documentation-only; does not modify schema contracts, validators, federation runtime behavior, signing semantics, or policy evaluator behavior.

## Purpose of the Foundation Layer

The HC:// Foundation Layer defines the constitutional and governance baseline that must remain inspectable before scaling runtime operations.

Its purpose is to stabilize shared meaning across these areas:

- **constitutional principles:** the durable principles that prevent hidden authority and preserve challengeable verification.
- **governance semantics:** explicit interpretation boundaries for maintainers, validators, reviewers, and public participants.
- **accountability philosophy:** trust is earned through attributable conduct, not role labels or claims.
- **trust taxonomy:** consistent trust language, trust states, and boundary terms across repository documentation.
- **audit continuity:** provenance and audit trail expectations remain traceable across trust-impacting actions.
- **federation oversight foundations:** independent cross-review expectations and anti-capture posture are documented before broad federation scaling.
- **public verification semantics:** public-facing verification meaning remains caution-aware, challengeable, and non-authoritarian.

## Definition: Foundation Stable Candidate

In HC-TRUST-LAYER, a **Foundation Stable Candidate** means the Foundation Layer is sufficiently settled for phased transition planning toward Operational Core development.

It means:

- foundational semantics are mostly settled and consistently documented;
- core governance principles exist with inspectable boundaries;
- accountability model exists with attributable role expectations;
- trust boundaries are documented across verification, governance, and public interpretation surfaces;
- public trust semantics exist for caution states, challengeability, and review routing;
- escalation paths exist for disputes, conflicts, integrity risk, and oversight routing;
- threat model exists and is maintained as a living, reviewable baseline.

A stable candidate is a **maturity checkpoint**, not a completion claim.

## What Remains Flexible After Stable-Candidate Declaration

Foundation stability does not freeze implementation direction. The following remain intentionally flexible under governance controls:

- operational implementation details;
- UI/UX evolution and mobile-readable public flows;
- federation scaling models and phased interoperability rollout;
- validator diversity and reviewer composition expansion;
- AI analysis model selection and explainability patterns;
- deployment architecture and environment-specific operations design.

These areas may evolve so long as advisory-only verification, human-supervised validation, and canonical boundary protections remain intact.

## Transition Criteria Toward Operational Core

Transition planning toward Operational Core should begin only when the following are stable in documentation and review practice:

- **stable trust semantics:** trust terms and caution-state meaning are consistent across key documents.
- **stable governance philosophy:** governance baseline rejects hidden control and preserves challengeability.
- **stable verification boundaries:** canonical and non-canonical boundaries remain explicit and enforced by guardrails.
- **stable audit expectations:** provenance continuity and audit trail expectations remain clear for trust-impacting actions.
- **stable dispute concepts:** dispute, escalation, and conflict-state handling concepts are documented and reviewable.

## Operational Core Focus (Next Layer)

After Foundation Layer stable-candidate conditions are met, Operational Core work should focus on bounded implementation of:

- real validator orchestration;
- federation synchronization pathways;
- public verification routing clarity;
- consensus coordination with visible disagreement handling;
- scalable audit infrastructure;
- trust scoring support surfaces;
- reviewer tooling and low-friction operator diagnostics.

All Operational Core increments should remain reversible, auditable, and routed through human-supervised validation for consequential trust interpretation.

## Stability, Change, and Evolution

Foundation stability in HC:// does **not** mean permanent immutability of all governance artifacts.

- constitutional changes should remain traceable with attributable rationale;
- governance evolution should remain inspectable through visible review history;
- trust systems must evolve with threats, incentives, and adversarial behavior.

The objective is not frozen governance; the objective is controlled, transparent, and auditable governance evolution.

## Architectural Progression

HC-TRUST-LAYER progression remains staged:

1. **repository** — documentation-first trust baseline, guard continuity, and canonical boundary clarity.
2. **protocol** — stable protocol semantics and verification interpretation boundaries.
3. **governance framework** — accountable review routing, escalation posture, and oversight clarity.
4. **validator ecosystem** — expanded validator/reviewer participation with attributable cross-review.
5. **distributed trust infrastructure** — phased federation-scale trust operations with preserved inspectability.
