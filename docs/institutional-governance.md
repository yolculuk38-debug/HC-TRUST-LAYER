# HC-TRUST-LAYER Institutional Governance Foundation

## Status

- documentation-only architecture foundation
- no runtime institutional governance implementation in this phase
- no institutional endorsement claim in this phase
- no autonomous governance claim

## Institutional Governance Overview

Institutional governance in HC-TRUST-LAYER defines how public-interest organizations, independent operators, and private entities can participate in verification infrastructure without collapsing governance boundaries.

The HC:// model treats governance artifacts as auditable process records connected to provenance, audit trail continuity, and canonical record references.

## Institutional Participation Concepts

Institutional participation can include:

- standards organizations contributing interoperability guidance
- archives and libraries preserving long-term verification evidence
- academic institutions reviewing methodology and risk assumptions
- civil-society groups providing transparency and accountability feedback
- regulated entities publishing policy-constrained verification disclosures

Participation is process-based and evidence-based; it is not an endorsement layer.

## Independent Validator Participation

Independent validator participation is necessary to reduce single-operator concentration in HC:// verification infrastructure.

Concepts:

- independent validators publish attributable verification rationale
- validator outputs remain tied to provenance and canonical record context
- validator disagreements remain visible rather than overwritten
- high-impact divergence can trigger human-supervised validation escalation

## Public/Private Verification Roles

Public and private verification roles can coexist when boundaries are explicit.

Public-role examples:

- public verification explorer publication
- open audit trail visibility
- transparent revocation and supersession notices

Private-role examples:

- internal compliance verification pipelines
- confidential evidence review paths with redaction controls
- policy-specific trust query routing not exposed publicly

Boundary rule: private process may exist, but public-facing outputs should preserve reproducibility signals where policy allows.

## Governance Boundary Concepts

Governance boundaries should define:

- who can propose policy changes
- who can approve or reject policy updates
- which decisions require multi-party review
- what evidence threshold is required per decision type
- where decision logs are published in canonical record form

Boundaries should be explicit and versioned to preserve audit trail continuity.

## Human Oversight Requirements

Human-supervised validation is required for governance-critical events, including:

- trust-anchor acceptance changes
- high-impact revocation disputes
- cross-federation contradiction resolution
- policy exception handling

Automation can assist triage, but final governance decisions should remain attributable to accountable human roles.

## Federation Governance Considerations

Federation governance in HC-TRUST-LAYER should preserve participant autonomy while enabling interoperable dispute visibility.

Considerations:

- local policy sovereignty with explicit federation disclosure
- shared minimum metadata for provenance and audit trail exchange
- no forced global consensus claim
- transparent mapping of federation participant governance posture

## Audit Transparency Concepts

Institutional governance should generate transparent audit artifacts such as:

- policy change logs
- validator participation history
- dispute outcome rationale
- revocation/supersession governance references

These artifacts should remain accessible for retrospective review and canonical record continuity.

## Dispute Escalation Considerations

Dispute escalation pathways should define:

- trigger criteria for escalation
- escalation tiers and required reviewers
- time-bound response expectations
- final decision publication requirements

Escalation should append history and preserve conflicting evidence for later review.

## Institutional Trust Risks

Key institutional trust risks include:

- opaque governance workflows
- uneven access to dispute channels
- inconsistent reviewer independence
- policy drift without traceable publication

Risk mitigation should prioritize transparency, reproducibility, and human-supervised validation.

## Centralized Control Risks

Centralized control risks include:

- single-operator policy capture
- unilateral revocation pressure
- suppression of dissenting validator evidence
- non-transparent changes to trust query behavior

Mitigation concepts:

- independent validator diversity
- public audit trail publication
- documented governance boundaries
- federation-level visibility for major governance events
